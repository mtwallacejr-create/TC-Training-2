Section 1

What Is the Stacked Method?

By the end of this module, you'll be able to explain the Stacked Method from every angle — buyer, seller, and investor — and walk through the deal mechanics, the people involved, and the math behind every closing.

The Stacked Method is the only real estate strategy where the BUYER walks away from closing with money in their pocket.

That's the headline — but every party in this deal wins in their own way. Three different perspectives are "stacked" into one transaction, and understanding each one is the key to understanding the whole strategy.

The Three Points of View
Buyer's POV
  • Walks away from closing with cash in hand for contingencies and emergencies
  • Faces less stringent underwriting requirements — the DSCR lender uses the property's tenant income to qualify the deal, not solely the buyer's personal income
Seller's POV
  • Receives a large down payment — bigger than in any other creative finance transaction
  • Gets a steady monthly income stream
Investor / Wholesaler / Contract Holder POV
  • Earns a massive assignment fee for putting the deal together
  • Because this deal type genuinely benefits both buyers and sellers, it's typically much easier to find people interested in doing one
It Takes Two Transactions

The Stacked Method isn't one deal — it's two deals layered on top of each other ("stacked"):

  1. The Purchase Agreement — the standard contract that transfers the property
  2. The "Seller Finance" — a second, separate financing arrangement between the buyer and seller
⚠ The Critical Rule

The lender on the first transaction can NEVER know about the second leg of the deal.

Why this is so critical: When a lender approves a loan, they're underwriting the buyer's full financial picture and confirming the property isn't carrying any hidden debt. A second, undisclosed loan changes both of those facts. If the lender discovers it — even after closing — they can call the entire loan due and payable immediately, deny funding altogether, or treat it as a misrepresentation on the loan application. One slip here doesn't just complicate the deal — it can unwind the whole transaction and create real legal exposure for everyone involved. This is the rule every TC has to protect at all costs.

Purchase vs. Cash-Out Refinance

Every Stacked Method deal is built on top of one of these two structures. Knowing which one you're in changes how the whole deal flows.

Purchase

Ownership transfers from the seller to the buyer. The seller is not responsible for getting a new loan — all of the seller's existing loans get paid off at closing.

Cash-Out Refinance

The seller and buyer form an LLC together within the first week of being under contract. That new LLC applies for maximum financing. Funds land in escrow, then get released as normal — a faster, simpler process overall.

Worth noting: because the seller is now a member of the LLC applying for financing, the "seller's debt" technically isn't paid off the same way as in a Purchase — instead, a different member of that company becomes responsible for paying it.

Pref-Eq vs. Seller Second

This is the heart of "what is the seller actually being protected — and protected from?" The seller finance leg can be secured one of two ways, and the choice changes who carries the risk.

Seller Second

A loan recorded directly against the property.

Safer for the seller: if the buyer ever sells, the title company will see this loan on record and must pay it off first.

Riskier for the buyer: the DSCR lender checks property records for up to 3 months — if they find it, they can make the entire underlying loan due and payable on the spot.

Security Docs Used
  • Note — promissory note: buyer promises to repay seller a specific amount on specific terms (principal, interest rate, payment schedule, balloon date)
  • Mortgage / Deed of Trust (DOT) — places the lien on the property and gets recorded with the county; makes the loan "against the property" real and searchable by title

Preferred Equity (Pref-Eq)

A loan recorded against the purchasing company — not the property itself.

Safer for the buyer: the DSCR lender checks property records for up to 3 months after closing, and since this loan isn't attached to the property, there's almost no chance it gets discovered or recalled.

Less secure for the seller: the only way to find this loan is to run a credit/background check directly on the company — exceptionally rare in practice.

Security Docs Used
  • Note — promissory note spelling out repayment terms
  • Amendment to Operating Agreement (OA) — updates the company's OA to reflect the lender's stake/claim (creates the seller's secured position against the company)
  • UCC Filing — public record of the lender's claim against the company (recommended, not required)
Key Term

UCC Filing — In plain terms, a UCC lien is essentially a loan recorded against a company in and of itself. A UCC filing is recommended, but not required. It gives the lender on a Pref-Eq position a documented, searchable claim against the company — extra protection without attaching anything to the property.

Key Parties

Every Stacked Method transaction runs through the same cast of characters. Learn these cold — you'll be coordinating between all of them.

PartyRole in the Deal
SellerThe current property owner who receives the down payment and holds the seller finance note. Their existing mortgage gets paid off at closing (in a Purchase) or via the LLC refinance (Cash-Out Refi).
Wholesaler / Contract HolderThe investor who put the deal together under contract. They assign their contract to the end buyer for an assignment fee, paid out of the Seller Finance principal balance at second-leg closing.
End Buyer / AssigneeThe person or entity taking ownership of the property. They obtain the DSCR loan, assume responsibility for the seller finance, and walk away from closing with cash in hand.
Title CompanyManages the first leg of closing — verifies clean title, handles the HUD/closing statement, disburses funds, and records the first-leg deed and mortgage documents.
Escrow OfficerWorks within the title company; the specific person who holds and disburses funds, prepares the closing disclosure, and coordinates the mechanics of closing day. This is your main title contact.
Broker / LenderThe DSCR lender who originates and funds the first-leg loan. They underwrite based on the property's rental income (DSCR). Critical: they must never see the seller finance terms.
AMC / AppraiserThe Appraisal Management Company (AMC) assigns an independent appraiser to value the property. The appraisal determines whether the DSCR loan amount is supportable. If the appraised value comes in low, a price addendum may be required.
Listing AgentThe seller's real estate agent (if one is involved). Their commission is paid from the Down Payment proceeds and must be notated on the PSA.
Closing AttorneyHandles the second-leg closing — prepares and collects the security docs (Note, OA Amendment / Deed of Trust), holds the escrow until second-leg docs are signed, and distributes assignment fees to consultants.
ConsultantsInvestors or referral partners who brought components of the deal (buyer, seller, funding, etc.) and are owed a piece of the assignment fee at second-leg closing.
Transactional FunderA short-term lender who provides funds to facilitate a double-close or bridge situation where the wholesaler needs to temporarily take title before assigning. Not used in a standard assignment.
EMD LenderA private lender who loans the Earnest Money Deposit to the wholesaler/buyer so they can open escrow without using their own cash. This loan gets repaid from the Seller Finance principal balance at second-leg closing.

Section 3

The Numbers (Deal Terms)

This section is about recognizing and checking deal terms — not full underwriting. You're learning to read the numbers, not build them from scratch.

What Makes Up the Purchase Price

Strip it down to the studs, and the Purchase Price is really just two pieces stacked together:

Down Payment to the Seller
+ Principal Balance of the Seller Finance
= PURCHASE PRICE
Key Term

EMD and Agent Commission do NOT change the Purchase Price. They're separate line items that ride alongside the deal — but both still need to be clearly notated on the PSA so every party can see them.

Where Each Piece of Money Actually Goes

Knowing what the Purchase Price is made of is only half the picture — the real skill is knowing where each piece flows once it lands.

From the Down Payment to the Seller:

  • All existing mortgage balances on the property are paid off
  • Closing costs (if the PSA shows the seller pays)
  • Agent commission (if the PSA shows the seller pays)
  • Prorations for security deposits and rent
  • Prorations for property taxes

From the Principal Balance of the Seller Finance:

  • All assignment fees are paid out
  • EMD loans get paid back
  • Credit sponsors get paid
  • Closing costs for the second closing attorney

The Process

Here's the Stacked Method from contract to close, step by step. We'll go much deeper into each one in Module 3 — for now, get the order locked into your head.

The 13-Step Process: Contract to Recording
1
Contract packages drafted
2
Contract signed
3
Open new file with Title / Lender
4
Submit EMD
5
Schedule appraisal
6
Review title report / clear title
7
Review appraisal (issue price addendums if needed)
8
Get CTC ("Clear to Close") from Title & Lender
9
Submit file to 2nd closing attorney
10
Close on the first leg — seller gets down payment, closing attorney gets escrow holdback
11
Close on the second leg — security docs signed, consultant assignment fees paid
12
Record first-leg documents
13
Record second-leg documents (90–100 days later preferred)
Why 90–100 Days for Step 13?

The DSCR lender actively checks property records for up to 3 months after closing. Recording the second-leg documents (the Pref-Eq OA Amendment, Seller Second Deed of Trust, or UCC) immediately would risk the lender discovering the seller finance position. Waiting until after that window closes protects the deal from being recalled or the loan being called due and payable.

Deal Initiation

Every deal opens the same way: LOI to test the deal, Cash PSA to lock it in. Know the difference between the two and when each is in play.

Letter of Intent (LOI) Initiation
What It Is

Non-binding proposal outlining key deal terms — purchase price, carry structure, timeline, and buyer entity — before a formal contract is drafted.

Used For
  • Opening negotiations on creative finance deals
  • Testing seller interest and establishing price/terms
  • Presenting Sub-To, Seller Finance, or Pref-Eq in plain language before committing to a full PSA
Key Steps
  • Fill property address and buyer LLC name
  • Input proposed purchase price and cash-to-seller breakdown
  • State seller carry terms (rate, balloon, payment if any)
  • Set proposed inspection and closing timeline
  • Send to seller; collect signature to confirm intent
Cash PSA Initiation
What It Is

Core Purchase and Sale Agreement for cash, conventional, or investor deals. The binding contract defining every term — purchase price, EMD, timeline, parties, and closing costs.

Used For
  • Standard wholesale assignments where end buyer pays cash or arranges own financing
  • All deal types as the baseline contract — addenda supplement it
  • Establishing the legal framework title uses to process the closing
Key Steps
  • Sections 1.1–1.3: Property address, APN, legal description
  • Sections 1.4–1.5: EMD amount and purchase price breakdown
  • Sections 1.6–1.7: COE date and escrow agent info
  • Sections 1.9–1.12: Inspection period, closing costs, occupancy
  • Signature page: verify all signatures, initials, and dates

Assignment & Cancellation

Exiting or unwinding a deal. Two outcomes, two documents — know which situation calls for which.

Assignment Agreement Assignment
What It Is

Transfers the buyer's equitable interest in the PSA to a new end buyer (assignee). Assignee steps into the buyer's position and closes the deal, paying an assignment fee.

Used For
  • Wholesale exit strategy — the primary way wholesalers monetize a deal
  • Collecting the assignment fee from the end buyer
  • Legally documenting the transfer of contract rights
Key Steps
  • Fill assignor and assignee legal names
  • Reference original PSA by property address and effective date
  • Enter assignment fee amount and payment method
  • State that assignee assumes all buyer obligations under the PSA
  • Both parties sign; send copy to title with original PSA

Cancellation Agreement Cancellation
What It Is

Mutual agreement terminating the contract and releasing all parties. Also directs the title company on how to disburse the Earnest Money Deposit.

Used For
  • Deal falls through — buyer cancels or parties agree to unwind
  • Releasing all parties cleanly with no remaining obligations
  • Directing EMD to the correct party
Key Steps
  • Fill property and names of all parties
  • Enter EMD amount and specify release instructions
  • Both parties sign — seller first, then buyer/assignee
  • Send signed cancellation to title company immediately
  • Confirm title company acknowledges and cancels escrow

JV & Partnership Structures

When multiple parties bring capital or deal components, a formal JV structure replaces informal handshake deals. Know the two types.

What It Is

JV structured as preferred equity. Investors provide capital and receive a fixed preferred return plus equity participation before profits are split.

Used For

Creative Finance Loan Agreements

2nd position, preferred equity, and SLA modifications. Know which structure matches which capital arrangement and who holds what security interest.

Subordinated Loan Agreement — 2nd Position Creative Finance
What It Is

Loan agreement where an investor provides bridge capital secured by a second-position lien, subordinate to the existing first mortgage.

Used For
  • Investor-funded "Morby Method" 2nd position deals
  • Providing the buyer capital to fund the gap between 1st mortgage and purchase price
  • Structuring the investor's security interest and repayment terms
Key Steps
  • Fill lender and borrower entity names
  • Enter loan amount (2nd position investment)
  • Set interest rate, monthly payment if any, and maturity date
  • Describe lien position and property as collateral
  • Both parties sign; lender's counsel records 2nd lien at closing
Preferred Equity Loan Agreement Creative Finance
What It Is

Loan structured as preferred equity — investor receives priority preferred return before other equity participates, often with an additional equity percentage.

Used For
  • Pref-Eq structures where investors want priority repayment + equity upside
  • Deals using preferred equity in lieu of a traditional loan or 2nd lien
  • Capital raises where the investor is an equity partner, not just a debt holder
Key Steps
  • Fill investor and borrower/deal entity names
  • Enter preferred return rate (annualized %) and equity participation %
  • Set loan principal, term, and maturity/exit trigger
  • Define default provisions and investor remedies
  • Both parties sign; attach to operating agreement for deal LLC
Addendum to SLA Creative Finance
What It Is

Modification document that amends terms of an existing Subordinated or Preferred Equity Loan Agreement — extensions, rate adjustments, or payoff modifications.

Used For
  • Extending maturity when a deal needs more time to close or refinance
  • Modifying interest rate or repayment structure on an active loan
  • Documenting any agreed change to original SLA terms
Key Steps
  • Reference original SLA by property and execution date
  • Clearly identify which terms are being modified
  • State new terms (new maturity date, rate, payment schedule)
  • Both original parties sign the addendum
  • Attach to original SLA; send copies to all parties and title

EMD Lending Documents

Third-party earnest money funding. Four documents govern this process — one to open it, one to evidence the debt, one to pay back the lender, and one to modify it if the timeline shifts.

Section 1

The Title Report

Documents you receive throughout the process — starting with the most critical one you'll handle on every deal. Learn to read it in either format, clear what blocks closing, and flag what kills deals.

What Is It?

The title report (also called a Preliminary Title Report or Title Commitment) is prepared by the title company after they search public records tied to the property. It tells you who legally owns the property, what liens or encumbrances exist, and what conditions must be satisfied before the transaction can close. As a TC, this is one of the most critical documents you will handle on every deal.

Key Term

Title Insurance — Once all items are cleared, the title company issues title insurance: a policy that protects against ownership defects not discovered during the search.

Cost

$200–$700. Usually built into the title/escrow fee — you typically won't see it as a separate line item. If a deal cancels after title is ordered, this fee may be deducted from the EMD. Know this before you order.

How Long Does It Take?
ScenarioTurnaround
Standard residential48 hours – 15 business days
Complex properties3–4 weeks (probate, multiple transfers, rural counties)
Expedited24–48 hours with an additional fee
■ TC Action Item

Order title the moment you open escrow. Do not wait.

Follow up daily until received. Every day you delay is a day you can't clear issues before the closing deadline.

How to Read It

Title commitments come in two formats depending on the underwriter and ALTA form version. Both contain the same information — just labeled differently.

FormatScheduleWhat It Contains
A / B / C
(3-schedule)		Schedule ATransaction details — parties, property, vesting, effective date
Schedule BRequirements to clear before close (what you must do)
Schedule CExceptions — what title insurance will NOT cover
A / B-1 / B-2
(2-schedule)		Schedule ATransaction details — same as above
Schedule B-1Requirements to clear before close (same function as Schedule B)
Schedule B-2Exceptions — what title insurance will NOT cover (same function as Schedule C)
Why Does the Format Differ?

It comes down to the underwriter. The ALTA 2016 Commitment form uses the A / B-1 / B-2 structure. Older or state-specific forms use A / B / C. You may receive either format in the same week. Don't let the labeling confuse you — the function is identical.

■ TC Action — When you open a title commitment for the first time
  1. Find the schedule with a numbered list of items you must DO or PROVIDE. That is your requirements list (Schedule B or B-1). Work from this first.
  2. Find the schedule listing easements, CC&Rs, and recorded documents the policy won't cover. That is your exceptions list (Schedule C or B-2).
  3. When in doubt, call your escrow officer: "Which schedule has the requirements I need to clear?"
The Three Schedules
Schedule A — About the Transaction

Confirms the facts of the deal. Look for:

  • Effective date (will be re-pulled before close — the final pull is what governs)
  • APN (Assessor's Parcel Number)
  • Type of policy: Owner's Policy (protects buyer) vs. Lender's Policy (protects lender — required on all financed deals)
  • Proposed insured — must match your contract exactly
  • Legal description of the property
  • Vesting — who currently owns it and how title is held
■ What to Check

Buyer name, purchase price, and legal description must match your contract exactly. Any mismatch must be corrected before close.

Schedule B or B-1 — Requirements (Your Action List)

Lists every condition that must be satisfied before title insurance will be issued. This is your working checklist. Nothing closes until every item here is cleared.

Schedule C or B-2 — Exceptions

Lists all exceptions — items that will NOT be covered by title insurance. Think of it as everything the title company is stepping around. Standard items (utility easements, boilerplate CC&Rs) are expected. Flag anything non-standard.

Common Title Items — Ranked by Frequency

These are the items you will encounter most often. Learn them in order.

1
Mortgage / Deed of Trust Payoffs
  • Any open mortgage must be paid off at closing unless the deal structure intentionally leaves it in place.
  • Always get a payoff statement with a good-through date of closing date + 14 days.
  • Use the Seller Information Form to collect lender info early.
2
Vesting Issues
  • Title is in the wrong name, an old name, or held incorrectly.
  • Common with estates, divorces, or LLCs.
  • Must be corrected via deed before or at closing.
3
Federal / State Tax Liens
  • IRS and state tax liens attach to all property owned by the debtor.
  • Must be paid or negotiated before close. Do not assume these will go away.
TC Action: Get a payoff. Confirm the release is recorded.
4
Judgment Liens
  • Court-ordered money judgments recorded against the seller.
  • Must be paid or released before closing.
TC Action: Get a payoff and confirm the release is recorded.
5
HOA Liens
  • Unpaid HOA dues can become a lien.
  • Call the HOA directly to confirm payoff — do not rely on what the seller tells you.
  • In super-priority HOA states (NV, CO, and others), HOA liens can jump ahead of a first mortgage. Know your state.
6
Open Permits
  • An un-closed permit means work was done without final inspection.
  • Title may require it be closed before issuing insurance.
TC Action: Contact the municipality and confirm status.
7
Probate / Estate Issues
  • Seller inherited the property or is selling on behalf of an estate.
  • May require probate court approval before sale.
TC Action: Escalate to the closing attorney immediately.
8
Marital Interest / Community Property
  • In community property states (TX, CA, AZ, NV, and others), a spouse may have an interest even if not on title.
  • Texas is a community property state — always check marital status on Texas deals.
9
Lis Pendens
  • "Suit pending." A recorded notice that the property is subject to active litigation.
  • Title insurance typically cannot be issued until resolved. Can stop a deal entirely.
TC Action: Flag immediately.
10
Easements & Encroachments
  • A standard utility easement usually doesn't affect the deal.
  • An encroachment (structure crossing a property line) is more serious — flag for buyer and attorney.

The Appraisal

The lender orders it, the lender controls it — but the TC drives the timeline and protects who sees the result. Know the two DSCR appraisal types and the low-value playbook.

What Is It?

An appraisal is a licensed appraiser's professional opinion of a property's market value based on condition, location, size, and comparable sales. On all financed deals (DSCR, conventional, private money), the lender orders and controls the appraisal. Your job: make sure it gets scheduled immediately and that results go only to the right people.

Cost

$500–$2,000 depending on property type and complexity. Almost always paid by the buyer — typically collected upfront by the lender before the appraisal is ordered.

How Long Does It Take?
StageTimeline
SchedulingA few days from lender order to appraiser visit
On-site inspection30 minutes to 2 hours depending on size and condition
Report deliveryTypically within 1 week of the visit
■ TC Action Item

Appraisal must be scheduled in Week 1.

If the lender has not confirmed an appraiser by Day 5, follow up immediately.

Types of Appraisals — Critical for DSCR Deals

On DSCR loans, the appraisal is about income potential, not just value. Know these two types:

Short-Term Rental (STR)

Values the property based on short-term rental income projections (Airbnb/VRBO model). Uses an income approach referencing STR market data.

Long-Term Rental (LTR)

Values the property using projected long-term rental income. Uses a rent schedule (Form 1007) alongside the standard appraisal.

Why It Matters

The type the lender orders affects the debt service coverage ratio (DSCR) calculation. Confirm which one is being ordered before the appraisal is scheduled.

What Happens If the Appraisal Comes In Low?

On a stacked deal, a low appraisal doesn't automatically kill the deal. Options:

  • Reduce the down payment
  • Reduce the principal balance or balloon amount on the seller finance note
  • Dispute the appraisal (requires comparable evidence — escalate to your investor)
■ Important

NEVER send the appraisal to the seller if the appraised value comes in higher than the purchase price.

Flag a low appraisal to your managing partner immediately. Do not adjust any figures yourself.

Section 3

The Operating Agreement

The governing document of the LLC buying the property. This one gets reviewed manually — no AI shortcut — and on a stacked deal, one mistake here can expose the whole structure.

What Is It?

The Operating Agreement (OA) is the governing document of the LLC that is purchasing the property. It defines ownership structure, member roles, voting rights, and how decisions are made. As a TC, you review the OA to confirm the entity is properly set up to close — and to flag issues before they become problems at the title company or with the lender.

Key Thing to Understand

OAs are living documents. They change frequently — lender requirements, credit sponsor additions, equity partner changes. Always confirm you have the most current version before submitting anything.

What to Look For
1
Who are the members?
  • Names and ownership percentages. Verify these match your deal summary.
2
Who has authority to sign?
  • This is the person who signs at closing. Look for: Manager, Managing Member, or the signature authority section — usually near the end of the OA or in the definitions.
3
Is the entity in good standing?
  • Confirm in the state where it was formed. If it's not in good standing, it cannot legally transact.
4
Foreign entity registration?
  • If the LLC was formed in a different state than where the property is located, it must be registered to do business in that state.
  • "Foreign" means formed in another state, not another country.
5
Restrictions on debt or liens?
  • Some OAs require unanimous member consent before the entity can borrow money or place a lien on the property. If so, all members must sign off before close.
6
Unanimous consent clauses?
  • If present, ALL members must approve major decisions. Flag these early.
■ ⚠ Preferred Equity Warning

If this is a stacked deal with a preferred equity partner, do NOT submit the OA to the title company or lender if the seller/equity partner is listed as a member pre-close.

Their membership interest should not appear in the OA until after closing.

Flag this to your managing partner immediately.

No AI Prompt for This Section

The Operating Agreement should be reviewed manually. It is a deal-specific legal document — no two are the same, and the nuances matter. AI can assist with general questions, but your eyes need to be on this document.

■ Your One Job on Every OA

Find the authorized signor. Look for: Manager, Managing Member, or the signature authority section. That person is who signs at closing. If it is unclear, escalate before the closing date — not the day of.

Section 4

Settlement Statements

The final financial accounting of the deal — every dollar in and out. On a stacked deal there are two of them, and you approve both before anything closes.

What Is It?

The settlement statement is the final financial accounting of a real estate transaction. It shows every dollar that flows in and out — who paid what, who received what, and what the net figures are for buyer and seller. As a TC, you review every settlement statement before closing to confirm it matches your deal terms exactly.

■ Stacked Method — Two Settlement Statements

On a stacked deal, there are TWO closings — and therefore TWO settlement statements:

• 1st Leg: The DSCR / purchase close
• 2nd Leg: The seller finance close

Both must be reviewed separately. Do not assume the second leg matches the first.

Two Formats You'll See

Combined HUD-1

Shows buyer and seller side by side on one document. No separate Debit/Credit columns — charges and credits are listed in columns by party.

Example: Elgin, TX — File AUS-2026-4410

ALTA Single-Party Statement

One statement per party (separate buyer statement and seller statement). Uses explicit Debit/Credit columns. Common with Qualia software.

Key Line Items Every TC Must Know

These appear on virtually every settlement statement. Know what each one is and what to verify.

1
Purchase Price
  • The agreed contract amount.
  • Must match your executed PSA exactly.
2
Earnest Money Deposit (EMD)
  • Appears as a credit to the buyer.
  • Confirm the amount matches what was deposited and acknowledged by escrow.
3
Closing Costs / Seller Concessions
  • Who is paying what.
  • Confirm against your contract terms — no surprises here.
4
Escrow Holdback
  • Funds held by the closing attorney or title company pending a condition (repairs, permits, etc.).
  • Must match your holdback agreement exactly.
5
Prorated Security Deposit / Rent
  • On tenant-occupied properties, the security deposit must be credited to the buyer.
  • Prepaid rent for the current month must be prorated between parties.
6
Listing Agent / Commission
  • Confirm amount matches the listing agreement or commission instructions.
  • Should appear as a debit to the seller.
7
TC Fee
  • Confirm your fee appears correctly and is paid to the correct entity.
8
Cash Due to the Seller
  • Seller's net proceeds.
  • Confirm this matches expectations based on your deal summary and executed agreements.
9
Cash Due from the Buyer
  • Buyer's total out-of-pocket at closing.
  • Verify against your deal summary and executed agreements.
